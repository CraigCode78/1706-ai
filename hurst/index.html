AI at Hurst

How I would approach it, as a question of leadership and change, and where the real value lies.

Craig Hepburn  ·  1706 Studio

A short note ahead of our conversation: who I am, how I think about AI at a school like Hurst, what we could bring, and a few areas worth covering when we meet. High level, and a starting point rather than a finished view.

01

Where I am coming from

I have spent my career where technology meets organisations that live or die on people, trust and reputation: senior roles at Microsoft and Nokia, and leadership work with UEFA and Art Basel. I now run 1706 Studio, an AI advisory. My work is transformation leadership: advising leaders on strategy and change as AI reshapes how organisations operate. I am also a Perplexity Fellow.

The name 1706 comes from the research paper that made today's AI possible. Its title was "Attention Is All You Need". I mention it only because it points at what matters here. As AI makes analysis cheap and abundant, human attention and judgement become the scarce and valuable thing. That is as true for a school as it is for any business.

02

What we do

We work with this technology every day, across organisations in almost every industry. That gives us something a lot of advisers lack: a clear sense of where AI creates value and where it fails.

We understand it at three levels that rarely sit together: the technology itself, including how these models work and where they mislead; the human side, how people actually adopt or resist change; and the business process and operations, where AI either changes how work gets done or becomes expensive theatre.

Part of that is staying close to what the frontier labs are building, and to the programmes, tools and funding now opening up for education, so an institution can take what is useful and ignore the rest.

03

The point of view

Strip out the hype and the technology talk and the story is simpler, and more serious, than the noise suggests. AI is not a technology question. It is a leadership and change question. It will change how a school operates, how staff spend their time, how work gets done, and in time how parents understand what a good education is. That it will change things fundamentally is not up for debate. Whether an institution leads that change or has it happen to it is.

Most schools will treat this as a technology problem and buy tools. The ones that win will treat it as a leadership problem and bring their people with them.

There are real benefits and real risks, and the only credible position is to be straight about both. The risks are not hype: serious research, including MIT's recent work on the cognitive cost of leaning on these tools badly, is starting to show them. But used well, the upside is just as real, and I have seen that close up.

04

Why this is not theoretical for me

I have two teenagers, both of whom went through Worth School and have largely taught themselves to use AI. I have watched it strengthen their learning rather than weaken it, which is a large part of why I believe the answer is to open this up well rather than shut it down.

I also hire and develop next-generation talent, and advise companies on the people they bring in. So I see directly what the AI era now asks of young people entering work, which is exactly the question a school preparing them has to answer.

05

Where I think the value is

If you do one thing well, it is this: decide what Hurst is now optimising for in a world where AI is everywhere, and lead from that single decision. Much of the complexity resolves once that is clear.

From there, most of the work is about people. The real blocker is rarely the technology; it is the confidence and capability of leaders, staff and parents. A school that can carry its community calmly through this, and speak plainly to anxious parents, holds the advantage. Alongside that sit two practical things: the quick operational wins, where AI reduces staff workload and cost and shows fee-paying parents their money is well spent; and the discipline of working with the right minds rather than inventing the approach alone. The direction of travel is already set, even the Department for Education and its own body, Oak National Academy, have built and backed AI tools for schools, so leading well means knowing whom to learn from, which programmes to tap, and which doors are real and which are just marketing.

There is also a prize in this. The schools that lead will define the approach others copy, and a method or tool that Hurst develops could in time become a revenue line as much as a reputation. That is for later, but the window is open now.

My part is to help your leadership team hold the strategy and the ownership, lead the change, and connect you to the right external minds and lab programmes, while avoiding the two traps I see most often: the evangelist who lets AI swallow everything, and the scattergun of initiatives that exhausts everyone and changes nothing.

06

For our meeting

A few high level areas that would be worth covering:

  • Where AI creates value for a school like Hurst, and where it does not.
  • Leading the change, and bringing staff and parents with you.
  • The quick operational wins that free up time and cost.
  • What employers now want from school leavers, and what that means for you.
  • The lab partnerships, tools and funding worth tapping into.
  • How to sequence it all without trying to do everything at once.

I have kept this deliberately high level. The substance is in the conversation.
