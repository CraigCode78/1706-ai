No. 01 · Visionix & Icare

A first read

Six observations on the marketing function at Revenio Icare, written in the days following our call and your note.

For Jussi Nevanlinna · Global Marketing Director, Icare From Craig Hepburn · 1706 Studio Date 8 June 2026 Status Working draft. Not for circulation.

Revenio is closing the Visionix transaction, more than doubling its addressable market and adding OCT as a strategic platform. You are building a new global marketing function across two heritage brands, three regional teams, and a clinical narrative that is already AI native while the marketing function is not.

Most marketing leaders inherit a function that needs improving. You are inheriting one that needs reimagining, four months in, with the cover of a major acquisition to do it. The question is not whether marketing will be rebuilt across this integration. It is who decides what it is rebuilt into.

Your note already does most of the diagnostic. What follows is a working note. A stance, six observations, a small synthesis, a view of capability, and a few questions for the session.

A note on posture

The strategy is yours, the function is yours to build. My job is to bridge between marketing decisions and AI capability. What is real, what is jagged, what is hype, what will actually work for your buyers and your team. The rest of this note is written from that position.

Stance

A team game.

Most AI transformations in marketing fail because they are done to teams rather than with them. People feel their work being automated, their roles being audited, their value being questioned. The defensive posture sets in fast and the energy goes. The approach behind everything that follows is the opposite of that.

The technology is real and the capability is genuine. But the transformation itself is a team activity. People shape it, live it, and own it. The appetite several of your team already have is the most valuable asset in the building. Top-down implementation kills it. People owning the change sustains it.

In practice this means confidence, air cover, and permission to experiment without consequence. Internal champions surfaced and given the platform to lead. New roles emerging from how people themselves want to evolve, not from an org chart redesign. The function changes because the people in it changed first, not because they were told to.

AI is a way to focus human attention on what matters. Storytelling, judgement, taste, relationships, the conversations that need a person in the room. The repetitive work goes to the agents. The work humans are uniquely good at gets more of their time, not less. Done well, the team feels more useful. Done badly, the team feels surveilled.

The technology is the easy part now. Culture is the hard part. Get this right and everything else in this document works. Get it wrong and even the best stack will be resented.

One more thing worth saying here. Everything that follows comes from the AI side of the table. Where the observations touch operational or strategic terrain that is yours rather than mine, treat them as patterns to test, not prescriptions to follow. The strategy stays with you.

Principle

The team is the transformation, not the target.

I  ·  The story discontinuity

The clinical product is AI native. The go to market is not.

A credibility gap forming between what Revenio sells and how it sells it.

iCare ILLUME uses artificial intelligence to detect early signs of diabetic retinopathy, age-related macular degeneration, and glaucoma. Revenio's own strategy frames the company as a provider of comprehensive eye care solutions built on AI-based software, not as a supplier of devices. The clinical narrative has moved.

The marketing function is part of the way there, but only just. Product marketing is largely not using AI beyond personal use, with the notable exception of your team lead. Marketing comms is optimising for AI search, Visionix.com has an AI assistant fielding first-line queries and guiding purchase decisions, customer service automation pilots have already cut volume by thirty-five percent, and an AI-based content engine is in build. Useful pilots. Real signal. But the function as a whole is not yet AI native. The gap between an AI native clinical product and a not yet AI native go to market is visible to anyone working in AI today. It will be visible to a wider audience as AI literacy spreads.

This reframes the work internally. The conversation with the board is not let's run an AI project in marketing. It is the marketing function is catching up with what Revenio Icare already is. That framing is more defensible, more urgent, and more interesting.

Principle

Marketing is the last function to look like the company.

II  ·  The closed loop opportunity

The European distributor model is the cleanest AI play in your stack.

Classical attribution cannot solve it. Inference can.

The European market is bifurcated. Most of the continent runs through distributors, which means the funnel is open by design. You feed it from many touchpoints but you cannot follow a lead all the way to a closed sale. In France, Germany, Italy and Iberia, where you sell direct and are looking to stand up SDR teams, the closed loop is buildable. In the distributor markets it is not. Classical attribution will not fix the gap. The data simply isn't there.

What modelling can do is build a high-confidence picture of qualified demand across the touchpoints you do own. Search behaviour, content engagement, trade event participation, account-level activity, geographic concentration, intent signals from owned channels. Lead scoring becomes probabilistic rather than deterministic, and in this context that is a feature, not a bug.

The second-order effect matters as much as the first. When the data story sharpens, the conversation with sales becomes a different conversation. That tends to be where AI investment in marketing produces its most material near-term return, in any business where the funnel is open or partial. How it plays inside your specific commercial structure is yours to read.

Principle

When you cannot close the loop, close the inference.

III  ·  The internal champion

The product marketing lead who wrote the strategy paper.

Your most important asset, mentioned almost in passing.

You noted, almost in passing, that your product marketing team lead has written a strategy paper on how AI should be used in diagnostics and screening. This is the most important asset in your team and the easiest one to overlook.

Internal champions matter more than external hires in this kind of transformation. They carry context, credibility, and existing relationships. The risk is that they get pulled into operational delivery and never get the air cover to lead the AI-native product narrative externally. The opposite is what you want. Bring them forward. Make them visible.

When I am with you, a thirty to sixty minute conversation with this person should be the first thing in the diary.

Principle

The transformation runs faster with one internal champion than ten external ones.

IV  ·  APAC as greenfield

The advantage of starting late.

A region without baggage is the advantage, not the gap.

In AI adoption, greenfield contexts are materially easier to build for than retrofit contexts. No legacy tools to integrate, no inherited team patterns to unwind, no assumptions to dislodge. The cost and complexity of AI first build is a fraction of AI retrofit. That pattern holds wherever you find it.

In your note, APAC sits in that category. A region with a marketing lead and no localised digital channels reads at first glance like a gap. Under an AI lens it reads more like an opening. The discipline, if you choose to act on it, is to resist replicating the European or US model. That would be laying copper, in your own framing from the call.

Whether you go after that opening, when, and how, is yours to decide. The AI principle holds either way.

Principle

Greenfield is the advantage, not the gap.

V  ·  Capability not capacity

Thirty is not the question. What thirty can become is.

A reframing for the board conversation that is coming.

Thirty people. Your instinct is that the function is too large because too much is being done classically. The instinct is correct but the framing matters.

The board conversation, when it comes, is much more powerful as we have reshaped what marketing does than as we have reduced headcount. The two can be the same outcome, but the second is defensive and the first is strategic. The path is to take each role and ask what becomes possible when the manual work goes away. A senior content lead spending sixty percent of their time on production is doing junior work. The same person, freed, spends that time on storytelling, narrative work, and senior conversations. Different role, different ROI, same person.

The cost story takes care of itself. Natural attrition, role evolution, and reallocation will reshape the team across the integration without a difficult announcement. That is the right pace for a listed company and the right register for the team.

Principle

Replace tasks, not people.

VI  ·  One engine, two brands

The integration window is the only window.

Decide the operating model before redistributing the work.

AI tools have collapsed the cost and complexity of operating centrally across regions. Content generation, localisation, lead nurturing, sales enablement, analytics, internal reporting. These can now run from one team for many markets in ways that were prohibitive two years ago. The unit economics of centralised operations look different now than they did before.

What this means for how a function is organised is a real question and a different one than it would have been before AI. Your instinct in your note that the US team could play a more global role has the technology behind it. Whether and how you act on that is yours to shape. The window in which big structural choices stay open tends to be narrow during integration. Worth knowing the constraint, even if the decision is not now.

Principle

The integration window is the only window.

Capability

What sits beneath.

The observations above only matter if the technology behind them is real. None of this depends on capability that is still emerging. All of it is achievable today with models and tooling that have been in production for more than a year. Worth naming the five capability classes that sit under everything, because the conversation moves faster when we are talking about the same things.

01

Language models that read, write, and reason.

Most of what people call AI in marketing right now sits here. The current generation reads documents, summarises calls, drafts content, writes code, and reasons about your business at near-human quality when given proper context. The bottleneck is not the model. It is the prompting, the data the model can see, and the design of the workflow around it.

Where it shows up here The Visionix AI assistant, the in-build content engine, and the upskilling work across the whole team.
02

Multimodal generation across asset types.

Text, image, video, and audio generated to production quality from a brief. The cost of producing marketing assets is falling by an order of magnitude every year. The production work your senior content lead carries today compresses from days to hours, with quality that holds for most of the channels you operate in. Not all channels, and not for everything. The direction is clear and the gap between what the team can produce and what an agency can produce is closing fast.

Where it shows up here The team reallocation question in observation V, and the broader cost story behind a thirty-person function.
03

Retrieval and discovery agents.

When a clinician, distributor, or analyst asks Perplexity, Claude, Gemini, or ChatGPT about tonometers, OCT systems, or eye care diagnostic platforms, your content either shows up properly structured or it does not. This is the agent-era search layer your team has not yet built for. The competitive risk is real and rising. Whoever in your category builds AI-readable product, clinical, and outcome data first will get surfaced disproportionately by the systems clinicians are starting to use as their first port of call.

Where it shows up here Observation I, the story discontinuity, and a competitive risk that does not yet appear in your note.
04

Workflow agents that execute tasks.

Not chatbots. Running software that does work that used to require a person. CRM enrichment, lead scoring, content distribution, competitive monitoring, calendar coordination, internal reporting, partner onboarding. Supervised, not autonomous. Not perfect. Real. The pattern is that one person plus a set of agents now does the work of three people six months ago. That is the practical mechanism behind the capability not capacity argument.

Where it shows up here Observation VI, where centralised agents serve regional teams, and observation V where they free people for senior work.
05

Modelling and inference on your data.

Finding patterns and predicting outcomes from your data that a human team cannot see on its own. Propensity scoring, intent modelling, attribution substitution where the classical chain is broken, account-level signal aggregation. The most technical of the five. The one with the most direct commercial impact in your near-term picture, because it is what turns the broken European funnel into something sales can actually act on.

Where it shows up here Observation II, the closed loop inference move, and most of the lead gen work across the whole function.

None of this is exotic. The barrier to any of it is institutional, not technological. Which is the entire point of this document.

For the session

Six questions.

Not for answering now. These are the ones I would want us to work through together when we meet, in the order they make the next ones answerable.

i

What does the CEO and the board need to see early in your tenure for this to be considered well started?

ii

Of the thirty people in the function, who already moves faster than the system around them?

iii

What does sales actually need from marketing in Europe to start valuing lead gen rather than dismissing it?

iv

How autonomous is the Visionix US team allowed to remain, and how autonomous does it want to remain?

v

Where does the existing in-house AI screening team sit organisationally, and could marketing collaborate with them on the AI-native content engine?

vi

What story are clinicians, distributors, and partners telling about Revenio Icare today, and what story do we want them telling next?

None of this is a finished view. It is a starting point to argue with, push back on, and shape together. Where I am wrong, the conversation gets sharper by being told. Where I am right, the value sits in turning observation into sequence and sequence into work.
