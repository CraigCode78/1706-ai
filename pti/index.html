01 The hypothesis

For most of computing history, we operated the machine. Now the machine operates on our behalf.

That is the real shift. Software has gone from something we control, click by click, to intelligence we hand a goal and let run. And as it does, the work it touches, producing, building, analysing, advising, is becoming close to free. We can make more, and faster, than ever before. So the hard question is no longer can we produce this. It is what should we point it at.

Few industries are better placed. The live moment is something AI cannot reproduce, which is why investment is pouring in, and almost everything around it is now open to be built better.

Part one

What is actually changing.

Three changes sit underneath all of it: intelligence got cheap, software can now act on its own, and it fails in ways that are easy to miss.

What an agent actually is

AI stopped answering questions. It started doing the work.

The important shift is from AI that responds to AI that acts. The first answers a question. The second does the task. These are agents.

Until recently
A clever assistant that answers.

You ask, it replies. A better search box. Useful, but it waits for you, does one thing at a time, and does nothing on its own. The work still sits with you.

Now: an agent
Software that takes a goal and gets it done.

You give it an outcome, not a question. It plans the steps, uses tools, reads and writes to your systems, and works through the task on its own, checking back when it needs you.

  1. Give it a goal: "sort this fan's complaint and offer a fair gesture"
  2. It looks up the account, the order, the policy
  3. It drafts the reply, applies the credit, logs the case
  4. It comes back only if something needs a human call

Why now: the models got good enough to plan and use tools, and cheap enough to run a task hundreds of times for pennies. An agent that costs almost nothing and can act on your systems is not a chatbot. It is a worker you can hand a job to. That is the change everything else follows from.

The economics · why now

The cost of intelligence is collapsing.

A model charges by the token. A token is a small piece of text, very roughly three quarters of a word, and you are billed for the text the model reads and the text it writes back. So every answer has a price, usually a tiny fraction of a penny.

That price has collapsed. The same answer that cost a dollar to produce in 2022 costs about a penny today. Two things drive it: the models keep getting more efficient, and the providers are in a price war. Few technologies have ever fallen in cost this fast.

What this actually changes.

When an answer cost real money, you only did it for the few things that clearly paid back. At a fraction of a penny, that limit falls away. The question stops being "can we afford to do this" and becomes "what is now worth doing that never was before."

In sport

Answering every fan question a club receives, all season, in every language, now costs less than one matchday steward's shift. Personalising every renewal email, scouting every lower-league match on video, briefing every sponsor weekly — none of these were ever impossible. They were just too expensive. That constraint is gone.

And it is no longer just corporate: nations now treat compute, chips and models as strategic assets, and, a wrinkle worth knowing, the leading open-weight models today are Chinese. As the models themselves turn into a utility, priced like electricity and available to anyone with an API key, the value does not disappear. It moves. Two piles, in opposite directions.

When intelligence is cheap, the scarce things are worth more, not less.

This is the map to keep in your head for the rest of the session. When the model becomes a utility, the question for any business is simple: which pile are you in, and what are you doing to move into the right one.

In sport

A rights holder starts with more of the scarce pile than almost any business on earth: the live moment, a rights-cleared archive, first-party fan data, and a brand people genuinely love. The risk is spending on the collapsing pile, generic content and rented tools, while the scarce assets sit ungoverned and leak.

Models, and the choices that matter

Not all AI is the same thing. The choices matter.

"AI" is one word covering two separate questions: what kind of model you use, and how you connect to it. They decide what you pay, how good the answer is, and who ends up holding your data. Neither is technical once put plainly.

First: what kind of model
Large models
How capable · the big ones

The frontier models. They handle almost anything and are the most capable, but cost the most. Right for hard, open-ended work; overkill for simple repetitive jobs.

For example: Claude Opus, GPT-5, Gemini Ultra.
Small models
How capable · the light ones

Smaller, cheaper, faster. For a narrow task they are often just as good at a fraction of the cost, and can run on modest hardware. In the last year they got good enough for most everyday work.

For example: Claude Haiku, GPT-5 mini, Gemini Flash.
Closed models
Who owns it · the provider

The model lives on the provider's servers and is never shared. The most capable models today are closed. You get access, not the model itself, and you use it on their terms.

For example: Claude, GPT-5, Gemini. You cannot download the model, only use it.
Open-weight models
Who owns it · you

The model is published, so you can download it and run it on your own systems. You can even train one further on your own data, so it learns your world while that data and that edge stay with you.

For example: Llama, Mistral, DeepSeek. You hold and run the actual model.
Then: how you connect to it

This is the distinction that matters most for a business, and the one most people skip. Whatever the model, there are two ways to use it.

Log in and chat

A person using the app

Someone opens ChatGPT or the Claude app, types, and the answer comes back. Quick and useful for one person, but the work is manual, one prompt at a time, and whatever you paste in goes up to their servers.

Wire it into your systems

The API, working on your data

You connect the model straight into your own software and data through its API. Now the intelligence comes down to your information, works on your facts, runs at scale and under your control. This is how you build something that is yours, not a tool a few staff happen to open.

A raw model is trained to be average.

This is the part most people miss. A model out of the box has learned from the whole internet, so it knows a little about everything and your business in particular not at all. Ask it something specific and it will give you a plausible, generic answer, and sometimes confidently invent one. That is not a flaw to be fixed later, it is what a general model is. The value appears when you wire it into your own data, systems and rules, so it answers from your facts rather than the average of everyone's. The model is the engine. Your context is what makes it yours, and makes it right.

The point underneath the choices.

A year ago, serious AI meant sending your data to someone else's system and hoping. Now you can run capable models privately and keep the data in-house. The most valuable thing an organisation owns is its proprietary knowledge. The model you choose decides whether that knowledge stays inside your walls or leaks into someone else's system.

In sport

Player performance and biometric data is among the most sensitive material a club holds, and regulators treat it that way. A small open-weight model running inside the building means that data can power the club's AI without ever leaving it. The model choice is a governance decision before it is a technical one.

Second-order effects · the market

Three things most people have not followed through yet.

The first-order changes are clear enough. It is the knock-on effects, one step further out, that reset how a business should think. Three about the outside world.

01

Your website is becoming an API for machines, not a destination for people.

Soon a large share of your "visitors" will not be people. They will be agents acting for people, fetching an answer and leaving. You stop designing for a human browsing and start designing to be the source a machine trusts and quotes.

When a fan's agent asks for kick-off, prices and availability, a club is either the source it quotes or a guess it makes. Almost nobody is working on being the source.
02

When everything can be faked, proof of real becomes a product.

As AI-generated content floods every channel, the scarce thing is proof that something is official and real. Your authenticated, signed "this came from us" content and data gains value precisely because everything else is suspect.

Verified highlights, signed stats, official player content: premium precisely because everything else can be faked. Authenticity becomes a technical and commercial asset.
03

The same force that disintermediates you lets you go direct.

Agents in front of you are a threat. But the same technology lets you collapse your own middlemen, reaching customers directly, globally, in any language, without the intermediaries who always sat in between.

A federation can now serve fans in forty languages, directly, without the intermediaries who always sat in between. Threat and opportunity are the same mechanism, pointed in different directions.
Second-order effects · putting it to work

And three about actually using it.

04

Anything you build on today's model is temporary by default.

Most of what you build on the current models will be outdated by the next one, in months. So the smart move is not the clever feature. It is to build the things that compound no matter what model sits underneath: your data, your relationships, your workflows.

Don't build the clever matchday chatbot. Build the clean fan data layer every future chatbot will need.
05

When an agent acts on its own, who is accountable?

An agent that takes actions, issues a refund, makes an offer, answers a season-ticket holder in your name, opens a gap the law has not caught up with. When it gets something wrong on its own, "the AI did it" is not a defence.

Before you let an agent act, decide who owns the outcome. Most have not.
06

The bottleneck is not capability. It is trust.

The models are already good enough to do far more than organisations let them. What holds adoption back is trust: will it be right, can I defend it, what if it is wrong. The winners are not those with the best AI, but those who build the checks that let them deploy it with confidence.

The game is not "get better AI." It is "get better at trusting AI safely."
The trajectory · held lightly

Where this goes next. And what nobody knows.

Anyone who claims certainty about AI's future is selling something. But the direction of travel over the next few years is reasonably clear, and worth holding.

Capability keeps climbing. Each model generation is meaningfully better than the last, and the curve has not bent yet. Plan on the assumption that whatever AI cannot do today, it may do within a year or two.

Agents get longer legs. Today an agent handles tasks that take minutes. The frontier is tasks that take hours and days, agents working in teams, checking each other's work, running whole processes end to end.

AI becomes continuous. The shift is from a tool you open to a presence that is always running: watching, preparing, acting within its limits, and surfacing only what needs a human.

And the honest unknown: nobody knows when the curve flattens. The credible positions range from a plateau within a few years to decades of compounding gains. The practical answer is the same either way: build the foundations that pay off in both worlds, your data, your workflows, your judgement.

Part two

The same force, from two directions.

One shift is inside the organisation: agents taking on the operating work. The other is outside it: AI changing how people find, decide and buy. Both are driven by the same cost collapse, and they arrive at once.

Shift one · inside the business

Agents are moving into the operating core.

The pattern, across every industry.

The first wave of AI was a person using a chatbot. The wave now underway is different: agents taking over repeatable operating work that used to need a team, running on their own, at any hour, for almost nothing. The pattern is the same in every sector: the routine, high-volume work goes first, and people move up to the parts that need judgement. It is happening now because the cost finally dropped low enough to make it worth doing.

Worth sitting with

A year ago, automating a task had to clear a real cost bar to be worth it. That bar has all but gone. So the question is no longer "can this be automated," it is "now that it costs almost nothing, what should be."

What agents take on first, anywhere
High-volume, repetitive tasks
Anything done from a rulebook
Drafting, summarising, first passes
Answering the same questions, at scale
Pulling data together into a report
Work measured in hours, not judgement.
The order is the same in every sector: the repeatable work goes first, people move up to the parts that need a human. As of late 2025, most organisations are still early, only around one in ten run agents in production, so this is the leading edge, not the average.

Shift two · outside the business

People are changing how they find and buy things. AI is the new front door.

How the habit is shifting, for everyone
Was: open a website, search, scroll, decide for yourself

Now: ask an AI, get one answer, increasingly let it act

Next: your own agent does the searching, choosing and buying for you
This is happening across consumer behaviour, not just sport. The interest in the thing itself has not changed. The doorway people come through has.

What this does to a club.

"What time is kick-off, can I still get tickets, what should I watch tonight." A fan used to type that into the club's app or site. Increasingly they ask an AI instead, and the club never sees the question. The lost visit is the small part. The real loss is the signal: who wanted what, when, in which language, at what price, the raw material of every personalised offer, renewal nudge and sponsorship pitch. It stops arriving without anyone noticing. And this is already live: you can buy inside ChatGPT, Google and Perplexity have their own checkout, and traffic from AI tools has grown many times over in a year.

It matters more in sport than almost anywhere, because when every firm can rent the same model, the one asset a rival cannot buy is your own first-party data on your own fans. Lose the direct relationship and you lose the only durable advantage you have.

Worth sitting with

When a fan asks an AI about your club tonight, what does it say, where did it get that, and do you have any say in it? Almost no organisation can answer that yet. The ones that work it out early will shape the answer rather than inherit it.

Part three

What it means for you, and for them.

One continuous move. What running this technology does to a firm like PTI, and why that is exactly what earns the right to advise a client on it. You cannot sell what you have not run yourself.

How PTI is built · people

The job is no longer to hire hands. It is to hire builders.

It is not “stop hiring.” It is hire differently.

The work that is shrinking is routine: research, synthesis, first-draft decks, the documents a junior used to produce. What is rising in value is the person who can build and ship with AI, not just describe a strategy.

It changes what every individual's job is. If agents do the execution, your people stop being doers and become managers of agents: their output is now their agents' output, and getting a good result depends on setting the agent up well. Everyone becomes a manager. The rarest and most valuable version of that person is not simply a coder. It is someone who first understands how the work actually gets done, and only then builds. That person has a name.

Action for PTI

An AI-native firm has three layers: people for judgement and taste, agents for execution, and a shared, machine-readable context layer that makes the firm legible to those agents. Hire people who understand operations first and build second, and invest in that context layer. Replace the missing junior tier with that capability, not headcount.

The role that makes it work

Someone has to sit inside the business and understand how it really runs.

Everything in this session, the models, the agents, the cost collapse, is abstract until one specific kind of person makes it work. Not a strategist with a deck, and not a coder handed a spec. Someone who embeds in the organisation, learns how the work actually gets done, and only then builds. In AI this person has a name: a forward-deployed engineer.

1
Understand the operation

Sit inside the business. Learn how the work really happens, where human judgement still matters, and where the repetitive system work could go to an agent.

2
Choose the right tools

Pick the models and tooling that actually fit the job, the small model where a small model will do, not the most expensive option by default.

3
Build the workflow

Wire the agent into the real process and the real data, so it does the work in context, not in a demo.

4
Test, evaluate, repeat

Run it against real cases, measure where it is right and where it is wrong, and keep tightening until it is reliable. This loop is most of the work.

5
Deploy with confidence

Only once it is accurate enough to trust does it go live in the organisation. Reliability earned, not assumed.

Why this is the part that matters.

Most AI projects stall not on the technology but because nobody did step one: they bought a tool and hoped, instead of understanding the work first. The understand-then-build order is what separates a system that gets used from a pilot that dies. And the test-and-evaluate loop is where confidence is earned: an agent that is right most of the time is not ready until that gap is closed.

Worth holding

The scarce skill is not knowing AI or knowing the business. It is one person who does both: understands the operation, then builds, tests and deploys until it works.

· Now take the lens into the client's room

Three things worth raising in a client's boardroom.

The same shifts from the first half, now concrete and specific to a rights holder. Three that tend to be missed.

1

Decide which AI assistants speak for the club, before they decide.

The front-door shift from part two, taken into the boardroom. The fan's questions now route through AI, and the assistants answering them are striking commercial deals today. The clubs that move early get to negotiate the terms: identity, consent, what data flows back, and the right to serve the fan directly. The ones that wait become a fact the AI repeats, on someone else's terms.

The board question is not "should we have a chatbot." It is "who is allowed to sell our tickets, answer our fans and represent our brand inside the AI layer, and what do we get back?"
2

Their data is locked in filing cabinets and people's heads.

Everyone now agrees data is the advantage. The catch is that most of what a club owns cannot be read by an agent as it stands. The valuable fan history sits in three ticketing systems that do not talk to each other; the commercial know-how is in a sponsorship director's head; the rest is in old PDFs and decks. An agent can only act on what it can read. So the real work is making the business legible to a machine: every process, every decision, every piece of knowledge in a format an agent can consume. That unglamorous translation, not the AI itself, is what turns "we have lots of data" into an actual advantage.

One Premier League club merged its scattered fan records into a single view. De-duplication alone turned 2.5 million messy records into about 2 million real, usable ones. Same data the club always had, finally in a state a machine, and the marketing team, could use.
3

They are sitting on assets that just became sellable.

Most clubs are treating AI as a cost to manage. The more interesting question is what they now own that AI has made valuable. Two examples. Their match and historical archive is rights-cleared, authentic footage, and AI systems badly need trustworthy, owned material to draw on, so it can be licensed rather than handed over for free. And when a fan's AI assistant starts buying tickets and trips on their behalf, that assistant is a new sales channel, one a club can strike terms with early instead of being routed through on someone else's terms.

The archive that has sat untouched for thirty years, and the AI agent that looks like a threat, are both potential revenue. The clubs that see that first will be selling something their rivals have not realised they own.
Governance and risk

Where the real risk sits. It is not the technology.

Most of what goes wrong with AI is not a model failing. It is data used without consent, content that should never have gone out, or a rule nobody checked. For a rights holder, four are board-level.

Data and consent

Fan data is a liability until it is governed

The data that powers personalisation is also the data that gets you fined. Consent, purpose and provenance have to be clean before any of it feeds a model, not after.

Likeness and IP

Players and AI-generated content

AI can now generate a player's face, voice and movement. Who owns that, who approved it, and what stops a sponsor or a fan making something you never sanctioned. This is live, and largely unresolved.

Regulation

The rules arrived before the maturity

The EU AI Act is in force, with real penalties, and biometric and performance data is treated as sensitive. "We did not realise" is not a defence a board wants to test.

Reputation

An AI speaking in your name

An agent that answers fans is the brand the moment it speaks. One confident, wrong, or off-tone answer at scale is a reputational event, not a support ticket.

None of this is a reason to wait. It is the reason to have someone independent in the room who treats governance as part of the build, not a box ticked afterwards.

Where to start

Nobody jumps to the end. There is an order to this.

The mistake is starting with the flashy agent. The sequence that works is unglamorous and it compounds. Most rights holders are at stage one. A small exercise as we go: tap the rung where each of your top three clients honestly sits.

Stage 1

Get the house in order

One clean, consented view of your data. Nothing useful happens with AI until this exists.

Stage 2

Use AI on yourself

Point it at internal work first: drafting, summarising, service. Low risk, fast learning, real savings.

Stage 3

Build one agent that matters

Pick a single high-volume job, build it properly, test it to reliability, deploy it. Prove it before scaling.

Stage 4

Make it a capability

Your own data layer, your own private models, new revenue from what you own. The edge competitors cannot rent.

The temptation is to skip to stage four because it sounds impressive. The firms getting value are doing stage one properly while everyone else demos stage three and stalls.

The terms, in plain words · a leave-behind
Model · the trained AI itself. A vast statistical engine that turns an input into the most likely useful output.
Token · the unit AI reads, writes and charges in. Roughly three quarters of a word.
Weights · the model's learned knowledge, stored as numbers. "Open-weight" means these are published, so you can run the model yourself.
Agent · AI given a goal, tools and permission to act. It plans, does the task, and comes back when it needs a human.
API · the connection that lets your own software talk to a model directly, rather than a person typing into an app.
Context · everything you give the model alongside the question: your data, documents, rules. The difference between a generic answer and yours.
Fine-tuning · training an existing model further on your own material, so it learns your world.
Hallucination · when a model confidently invents something plausible but false. A property of how models work, managed by grounding them in real context.
